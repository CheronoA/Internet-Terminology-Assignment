✍️ Web 2.0 — The Read-Write Web (2004-2016)

Web 2.0 changed everything. It turned the internet from a place where you could only read into a place where you could write, share, and interact. This is the web I grew up with — Facebook, YouTube, Instagram, Twitter, Wikipedia. Web 2.0 made you the creator, not just a consumer.

✏️ My simple way to think about it: Web 1.0 was like reading a newspaper. Web 2.0 is like having a conversation. You can post your own thoughts, comment on others, share videos, like photos, and collaborate with people around the world. You're part of the conversation, not just watching from the sidelines.

🔑 Key Characteristics of Web 2.0

  • Read-Write — anyone can create and share content, not just read it
  • User-generated content — YouTube videos, Instagram posts, tweets, blog comments
  • Social interaction — likes, shares, follows, comments, direct messages
  • Centralized platforms — big companies (Facebook, Google, Twitter) control your data
  • Rich user experience — AJAX made pages dynamic (no more full page reloads)
  • APIs — apps can talk to each other (embed a tweet, log in with Google)

📋 How Web 2.0 Changed My Daily Life

  • Instead of just watching TV — I watch YouTube creators and comment on videos
  • Instead of reading news — I scroll Twitter and see what people are talking about
  • Instead of asking friends in person — I post a question on Reddit or Facebook groups
  • Instead of writing in a diary — I post on Instagram or write tweets
💭 What I think: Web 2.0 is basically my entire internet experience. I don't remember a time without social media, comments, or sharing. But it's not all good — Web 2.0 also brought centralized control (big companies owning our data), echo chambers, and misinformation. That's why Web 3.0 is trying to fix some of those problems.

⚠️ The Downsides of Web 2.0

  • Data privacy — companies collect and sell your personal information
  • Centralized control — Facebook or Twitter can ban you or delete your content
  • Echo chambers — algorithms show you what you already agree with
  • Misinformation — fake news spreads faster than real news
  • Addiction — endless scrolling, notifications designed to keep you hooked
